History of Robotics

Robots are machines that can sense, think and act autonomously or semi-autonomously. They can perform tasks that are difficult, dangerous or tedious for humans, or simply provide entertainment and companionship. The history of robots is a fascinating journey from ancient myths and legends to modern science and technology.

Ancient Origins

Left: Karel Čapek,
Right: Cover of the first edition of the play Rossumovi Univerzální Roboti (Rossum's Universal Robots)

The word "robot" comes from the Czech word "robota", meaning "forced labor". It was first used in a 1921 play by Karel Capek, called R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots), which depicted artificial humanoids that rebelled against their creators. However, the concept of artificial beings that resemble humans or animals dates back much further.

Many ancient cultures had stories of artificial creatures that were made by gods, magicians or craftsmen. For example, in Greek mythology, Hephaestus, the god of fire and metalworking, created mechanical servants and warriors out of gold and bronze. In Chinese folklore, Lu Ban, a legendary carpenter and inventor, made wooden birds that could fly and mechanical imitations of animals and demons. In Indian Buddhism, there are tales of automata that recited sacred texts and wept when Buddha died.

These stories reflect the human imagination and curiosity about the possibility of creating life-like machines. They also raise ethical and philosophical questions about the nature and purpose of such creations, and their relationship with their creators and other beings.

Industrial Revolution

The history of modern robotics begins with the industrial revolution, which ushered in an era of complex mechanics and the use of electricity. This, in turn, led to the invention of power machines with small compact motors. Some of the earliest examples of such machines were automata: self-operating devices that performed predetermined actions or movements.

Left: Mechanical Turk Reconstruction
Right: A cross-section of mechanical turk

One of the most famous automata was the Mechanical Turk, a chess-playing machine that was built by Wolfgang von Kempelen in 1770. It consisted of a wooden cabinet with a chessboard on top, and a life-sized figure of a turbaned man holding a pipe. The machine could play chess against a human opponent, and even win. However, it was later revealed that the machine was actually a hoax: there was a hidden human operator inside the cabinet who controlled the movements of the figure and the chess pieces.

A Jacquard loom with punched cards

Another remarkable automaton was the Jacquard loom, invented by Joseph Marie Jacquard in 1801. It was a weaving machine that used punched cards to control the patterns of the fabric. The punched cards represented binary codes that instructed the machine which threads to use and how to move them. The Jacquard loom was one of the first examples of programmable machines, and influenced later developments in computing.

20th Century

Issac Asimov (left). A model of robot head from I Robot (right)

The 20th century saw rapid advances in science and technology that enabled the development of more sophisticated and intelligent robots. The term "robotics" was coined by Isaac Asimov, a science fiction writer and scientist, in 1941. He also formulated the Three Laws of Robotics, which are ethical rules for robots to follow

  1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
  2. A robot must obey orders given by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.
  3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

Asimov's stories explored the potential benefits and challenges of human-robot interaction, as well as the social and moral implications of artificial intelligence.

George Devol (right) and Joseph Engelberger served by the robot

The ﬁrst patent for what we would now consider a robot was ﬁled in 1954 by George C. Devol and issued in 1961. They were fixed machines that could perform repetitive tasks such as welding, painting or assembling parts. The device comprised a mechanical arm with a gripper that was mounted on a track and the sequence of motions was encoded as magnetic patterns stored on a rotating drum. They were controlled by pre-programmed instructions or by remote operators. The ﬁrst robotics company, Unimation, was founded by Devol and Joseph Engelberger in 1956 and their ﬁrst industrial robot was called Unimate, which was installed at a General Motors plant in 1961. It was a robotic arm that could move metal parts from one place to another.

The Unimate Mechanical Arm working at General Motors factory

The original vision of Devol and Engelberger for robotic automation has become a reality and many millions of arm-type robots 5 have been built and put to work at tasks such as welding, painting, machine loading and unloading, electronic assembly, packaging and palletizing. The use of robots has led to increased productivity and improved product quality. Today many products we buy have been assembled or handled by a robot.

Shakey(left) Lunokhod 1(right)

The first mobile robots were also developed around this time. They were autonomous or semi-autonomous vehicles that could navigate their environment using sensors and algorithms. Some examples are Shakey, which was built by Stanford Research Institute in 1966, and Lunokhod 1, which was sent by the Soviet Union to explore the moon in 1970.

Asimo

The first humanoid robots were also created in this period. They were robots that resembled humans in appearance and behavior. They could walk, talk, gesture and express emotions. Some examples are WABOT-1, which was built by Waseda University in Japan in 1973, and ASIMO, which was built by Honda in 2000.
